Unique, but Unoriginal

Often times the term original gets used colloquially to mean unique; however, they have distinctly different meanings. Originality refers to the origins of a work while uniqueness refers to its one-of-a-kind nature. Originality is a myth. There are no original works. Everything's a Remix. Likewise, uniqueness is common place: there's no such thing as a special snowflake. At best there are non-generic works, but they're not effectively original. They still owe a debt to their origins.

In STEM fields building on one's predecessors is accepted and understood: You study, study, study, then combine and build on the scientific facts you know. The same is true of creative fields; though, they'd be reluctant to admit it. You study generas, tropes, cliches, themes, and other artistic methods. Then, you build on the accumulation of culture before you.

There's no shame in being (non-colloquially) unoriginal. What's rubbish is genericism: The gradual destruction of culture through repetition of non-coherent and uncritical garbage. Everything uses sources, but not everything has to be the same. Combining diverse artistic methods with interesting – even if unoriginal – ideas can lead to a rich culture with quality works.


Freedoms

Intellectual property prevents alternative economic choices. Choices are kinda very important to freedom. Additionally, it discourages derivative forms of speech via ©ensorship. It also puts a clear economic gradient on information access, which should probably be a common good rather than a luxury.
